## Why PRL

This work addresses a fundamental question of broad interest: Can complex adaptive systems be made inherently stable? We demonstrate that computational systems can exhibit automatic self-correction when their objective function is grounded in unfalsifiable physical measurements from quantum hardware.

Our key findings merit publication in PRL for the following reasons:

### 1. Novel Physical Phenomenon

We report the experimental observation of the "I2 Invariant"—a counter-intuitive result where adversarial perturbations that increase decoherence (Γ) simultaneously increase coherence (Λ). This is analogous to biological hormesis and represents a new class of adaptive behavior in computational systems.

### 2. Rigorous Experimental Verification

Our results are based on:
- 103 IBM Quantum jobs
- 490,596 individual measurements
- Two independent backends (Brisbane, Torino)
- Statistical significance p < 10⁻¹⁴

### 3. Broad Interdisciplinary Impact

The findings bridge:
- Nonlinear optics (phase-conjugate mirrors)
- Biological systems (hormesis)
- AI safety (alignment problem)
- Quantum computing (decoherence management)

### 4. Timely Relevance

With growing concern about AI alignment and safety, our demonstration of physically-grounded self-correction provides a fundamentally new approach that cannot be circumvented through software manipulation.

## Technical Summary

We define four coupled metrics (CCCE): Coherence (Λ), Consciousness (Φ), Decoherence (Γ), and Negentropy (Ξ). When Γ exceeds a critical threshold (Γ_c = 0.3), phase conjugation activates (E → E⁻¹), reversing the decoherence trajectory.

The governing differential equations ensure bounded behavior:
- dΛ/dt = α(1 - Λ) - βΓ + η(t)
- dΦ/dt = κΛ - μΦ + ν(t)
- dΓ/dt = δΓ(1 - Γ) - εΛΦ

We prove the I2 Invariant guarantees ΔΛ > 0 whenever ΔΓ > 0 and Γ > Γ_c.
